What Nobody Tells You About Building a Marketing Department from Scratch

Nicole Summerlin June 2026 9 min read

I have built marketing departments from the ground up more than once. Each time, the external circumstances were different — the industry, the company size, the team, the budget. But the internal challenges followed a remarkably consistent pattern.

Not because companies make the same mistakes. Because the assumptions that lead to those mistakes are almost universal.

This is what I wish someone had told me before I started. And what I would tell any leader — whether you are the one being hired to build the function, or the executive who is bringing someone in to do it.

The most effective marketing departments do not live in a state of reactivity. They live in a state of long-term, compounding initiatives that layer on themselves over time and become more powerful as they mature.

Misconception One

You Need to Build the Team Before You Build the Foundation

The most common mistake I see marketing leaders make when tasked with building a department is moving too quickly toward hiring. It feels productive. There is pressure to show momentum. Leadership wants to see a team forming. And so the marketing leader posts job descriptions before they have done the foundational work that would tell them what they actually need.

Hiring before you have an intimate understanding of the company — its brand, its customers, its internal operations, its cross-functional dynamics, and where it genuinely sits in the market today — is one of the most expensive mistakes a marketing leader can make. The deeper cost is strategic: misaligned roles, confused job descriptions, an unstable foundation that cannot support growth, and marketing outputs that are scattered because the inputs were never defined.

Sometimes the answer is that foundational work needs to happen before a single marketing hire is made. That is not a failure. That is clarity. And it is far more valuable than a team assembled too quickly around the wrong objectives.

Misconception Two

If You're Not Tracking Everything, You're Missing Everything

If the leader building the department does not have a clear understanding of which numbers matter, which numbers influence strategy, and which numbers are directly tied to high-level business goals — then the department is operating without a compass.

Not every metric matters. Not every KPI deserves a dashboard. The discipline of building a marketing function includes deciding early and deliberately which measurements will actually tell you something useful — and which ones will simply create the appearance of progress without generating insight.

Metrics that inform strategy are different from metrics that justify activity. When a marketing leader cannot articulate the connection between their team's activity and the organization's goals, trust erodes. And in the early stages of building a department, trust is the most important resource you have.

Misconception Three

Great Marketing Produces Results Immediately

This one belongs to both sides of the table. Some marketing leaders overpromise in an effort to justify the investment in building the function. And some executives hire a marketing leader expecting that the leads, the press coverage, and the brand recognition will arrive shortly after the offer letter is signed.

Neither position serves the organization. The most effective marketing departments do not live in a state of reactivity. They live in a state of long-term, compounding initiatives — campaigns, content, and brand-building work that layers on itself over time and becomes more powerful as it matures.

The leaders and organizations who understand this build marketing functions that outlast market cycles. The ones who don't find themselves rebuilding from scratch every eighteen months — and wondering why nothing ever seems to stick.

The Challenge Nobody Talks About

Your Team Needs Clarity You Don't Always Have

The hardest part of building a marketing department is not picking the right tools. It is not finding the right people. The hardest part is providing clarity and direction to your team when you yourself are not always given clarity from above.

This is not a dysfunction. It is a normal condition of organizational life, especially in growing companies where strategy is still being formed at the top. But the marketing leader's job — one of the most important and least discussed parts of the job — is to thrive in that ambiguity. To synthesize what leadership wants and needs, even when it is not directly stated. To translate that synthesis into tangible, actionable direction for the team. And to simultaneously bridge the gap back to leadership so the entire organization moves in the same direction.

The leaders who do this well become indispensable. They are not just running a marketing department. They are functioning as connective tissue between vision and execution across the entire organization. The related challenge is building a team and a set of processes that can handle rapid, unexpected shifts in strategy. Because those shifts will come. The question is not whether your team can survive them. It is whether you have built the kind of department that can absorb a fast pivot without losing its footing.

What I Wish I Had Known

The Most Important Work Looks Like Doing Nothing

If I could go back and tell myself one thing before I started building my first marketing department, it would be this: make the time to sit and think.

In the early days, I did not feel like I could afford that luxury. There was too much to do, too many decisions to make, too many people counting on direction. An uninterrupted hour felt irresponsible when the work was piling up.

But not giving myself the space to think big — to step outside of the execution and look at the whole — kept me and the department in a constant cycle of doing. Always doing. Never quite elevating. The most important thinking I have done as a marketing leader did not happen in a meeting or a strategy session. It happened in the quiet moments I finally allowed myself to have. The ones where I was not managing tasks or managing people, but simply thinking about what we were building and why.

Protect that time. It is not a luxury. It is the work.

A Note for the Executives in the Room

If you are reading this as a leader who is thinking about hiring someone to build your marketing function, I want to leave you with one thought.

The marketer you bring in to build your department will only be as effective as the environment you create for them. Give them time to understand the organization before they hire. Give them clear alignment on what success looks like and the patience to let the work compound. Give them access to cross-functional leadership. And give them the psychological safety to tell you when the foundation needs work before the structure can go up.

The marketing leader's job is to build something that outlasts their first campaign. Your job is to make sure they have what they need to do it.
